Explore Tree Species along the Corridor

Discover the variety of tree species that enrich our green spaces.

Alder

  • Latin name: Alnus glutinosa
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Wind
  • Abundance: 1

Common in wetlands and by watercourses, Alder is crucial for stabilizing riverbanks and supports a diverse range of wildlife.

Common Ash

  • Latin name: Fraxinus excelsior
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Wind
  • Abundance: 2

Known for its height and slender beauty, Ash trees are also valued for their strong, elastic wood used in various applications.

Elder

  • Latin name: Sambucus nigra
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Animal
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 3

Elder is highly revered for its flowers and berries, which are used to create syrups, liquors, and natural remedies.

Goat Willow

  • Latin name: Salix caprea
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 6

Often found in woodland and scrub areas, Goat Willow is important for local wildlife, especially as a caterpillar food plant.

Green Ash

  • Latin name: Fraxinus pennsylvanica
  • Origin: Exotic
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Wind
  • Abundance: 1

Adaptable to various environments, Green Ash is prominently used in urban settings for its resilience and shade-providing canopy.

Hawthorn

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Crataegus monogyna
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Animal
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 22

Celebrated for its protective thorny branches and attractive spring blossoms that are beneficial to many insects.

Holly

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Ilex aquifolium
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Animal
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 2

Adorned with iconic red berries and glossy evergreen leaves, a classic symbol of winter and Christmas.

Horse Chesnut

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Aesculus hippocastanum
  • Origin: Exotic
  • Seed dispersal: Animal
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 1

Famous for its conker-bearing capabilities and candle-like spring flowers, a favorite in parks and schoolyards.

Red Birch

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Betula albosinensis
  • Origin: Exotic
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Wind
  • Abundance: 9

Notable for its striking reddish bark that peels in fine horizontal strips, adding winter color to landscapes.

Rowan

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Sorbus aucuparia
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Animal
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 1

Often planted for its ornamental qualities, featuring clusters of bright red berries and vibrant autumn colors.

Scots Elm

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Ulmus glabra
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Wind
  • Abundance: 2

Distinguished by its rough leathery leaves and resistance to Dutch elm disease, ideal for larger landscapes and parks.

Sycamore

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Acer pseudoplatanus
  • Origin: Exotic
  • Seed dispersal: Wind
  • Pollination: Insect
  • Abundance: 10

Fast-growing and hardy, known for its broad, maple-like leaves and helicopter seed pods that spiral to the ground.

Yew

Quick Facts:

  • Latin name: Taxus baccata
  • Origin: Native
  • Seed dispersal: Animal
  • Pollination: Wind
  • Abundance: 1

Ancient and revered, yew trees are often found in churchyards and are notable for their long lifespan and toxic seeds.
